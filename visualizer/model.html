A Dynamical Systems Model of Habit Formation

H-M-E-I-C State Space Model with Literature-Grounded Dynamics

Overview

This model treats habit formation as a trajectory through a 5-dimensional state space. Success and failure emerge from the geometry of the space -- there are stable attractors (the sedentary state, the established habit state) separated by a boundary called the separatrix. Your trajectory through this space depends on your starting conditions, the events life throws at you, and crucially, the complexity of the behavior you're attempting.

The core thesis: Start with the simplest possible version of the behavior. This keeps you below the separatrix, allowing habit strength to accumulate even as motivation naturally decays. Once automaticity is established, complexity can gradually increase. This is the bootstrapping strategy.

State Variables

H Habit Strength

How automatic the behavior has become. Ranges from 0 (no habit) to ~0.8 (highly automatic). Grows asymptotically when behavior occurs -- early repetitions matter most, with diminishing returns over time. The median time to reach 95% of the automaticity plateau is 66 days, ranging from 18 to 254 days depending on behavioral complexity. Lally et al. (2010), European Journal of Social Psychology

H decays when behavior stops, but follows a nonlinear trajectory -- rapid initial drop, then stabilization at a residual floor. Old habits never fully reach zero. Edgren et al. (2025), Applied Psychology: Health and Well-Being

M Motivational Activation

The fast variable -- the current "fuel" for goal-directed behavior. M decays hyperbolically (steep initial drop, long tail), not exponentially. This matches temporal motivation theory and delay discounting research. Steel & Konig (2006), Academy of Management Review

M is boosted by external events (health scares, social encouragement, New Year's resolutions) and by successful behavior (reward prediction error). It's drained by effort, stress, and indulgent fantasy. The key insight: once H is high enough, behavior continues even when M is low -- this is why habits survive motivation loss.

E Expectation Excess

The gap between what you expect and what's happening. Positive E means your expectations exceed reality ("I should be seeing results by now"). This variable drives two critical failure modes:

False Hope Syndrome: When E goes very negative (expectations crushed by reality), M collapses. The cycle repeats: recommit → inflate expectations → fail to meet them → crash → recommit. Polivy & Herman (2000, 2002), Current Directions in Psychological Science

The "What the Hell" Effect: When E goes very positive (perceived goal violation), M collapses catastrophically. One perceived failure triggers complete abandonment. Cochran & Tesser (1996)

I Identity Coherence

The slowest variable in the system (months to years). Represents the degree to which "I am someone who exercises" is part of your self-concept. Identity only grows when behavior is both occurring AND habitual -- you don't form an exerciser identity from a single gym visit.

Once established, identity provides a restoring force on motivation (preventing total collapse) and raises the habit ceiling. Exercise identity is the strongest predictor of closing the intention-behavior gap (d = 0.73). Rhodes, Kaushal & Quinlan (2016), Health Psychology Review; Berkman et al. (2017), Psychological Inquiry

C Behavioral Complexity

How demanding the target behavior is. A daily 15-minute walk is C ≈ 0.2; a 6-day gym split with meal prep is C ≈ 0.9. Unlike the other variables, C was traditionally treated as a fixed parameter. Our model makes it dynamic: it adapts based on success and failure feedback.

C has an equilibrium that depends on H: the sustainable complexity equals what your current habit strength can support. The daily walker (H≈0.7, C≈0.2) and the 3x/week gym-goer (H≈0.6, C≈0.5) are genuinely different equilibria, not the same attractor viewed from different initial conditions. 

C_equil(H, I) = C_min + (C_max - C_min) × H × (0.7 + 0.3 × I)

The Behavioral Trigger

Behavior B is computed from two competing systems, reflecting the neuroscience of goal-directed vs habitual control: Daw et al. (2005), Nature Neuroscience; Dolan & Dayan (2013), Neuron

Goal-directed component: A sigmoid function of M vs the behavioral threshold θ = C × (1 - H). When H is low, you need high M to act.

Habitual component: H itself drives behavior, but still requires minimum M. Human habits are fragile -- five experimental attempts to produce outcome-insensitive habits in humans all failed. de Wit et al. (2018), J. Experimental Psychology: General

The two components blend nonlinearly: at low H, behavior is mostly goal-directed; at high H, mostly habitual. A global activation gate ensures B = 0 when M is negligible -- you don't accidentally exercise.

The H-C Phase Portrait

The most revealing visualization of the model is the H-C phase portrait, which shows the landscape of possible trajectories in Habit-Complexity space.

The portrait reveals four distinct regions:

Stable Habit Basin (lower-right): High H, C matched to H via the equilibrium curve. Trajectories converge along the equilibrium manifold. This is where successful habit formers end up.

Complexity Trap (upper-left): High C, low H. The behavioral threshold is too high for available M to sustain behavior. Trajectories slide left toward sedentary. This is Sam's 6-day PPL split, Mei's half marathon plan -- too much too soon.

Sedentary Attractor (lower-left): A true fixed point. No behavior, no habit growth, no identity formation. Stable because the activation gate prevents behavior when M is negligible.

The Separatrix: A sharp boundary between the success and failure basins. Starting above this line in C (for a given M) leads to failure; below leads to success. The separatrix's position depends on initial M -- higher motivation allows higher starting C.

The bootstrapping strategy is geometrically equivalent to starting below the separatrix and riding the green trajectories into the stable habit basin. Simplify events move a red (failing) trajectory downward across the separatrix into the green basin.

Key Dynamics

Habit Growth (Asymptotic)

dH/dt = alpha_H × B × (A(I) - H) - decay_rate × (1-B) × (H - H_min)

Growth slows as H approaches the ceiling (set by identity). Decay accelerates when behavior stops at high H (use-it-or-lose-it). The residual floor H_min means old habits never fully vanish.

Motivation Decay (Hyperbolic)

dM/dt = -beta_M × M / (1+M)  +  reward  -  effort  -  indulgence  -  collapse  +  identity  +  social

Motivation is sustained by a balance of forces: reward from behavior (diminishing with habit strength), identity restoring force, social support floor, and habitual maintenance -- versus decay, effort cost, indulgence drain, stress, and potential collapse dynamics.

Complexity Adaptation (Equilibrium-Based)

dC/dt = -alpha_C_down × overreach  +  alpha_C_up × underreach  +  identity_pull

When C exceeds what H can support AND behavior isn't happening, C drops (learning to simplify). When C is below what H can support AND behavior IS happening, C rises (progressive overload). Social support accelerates both directions.

The Indulgence Mechanism

When motivated but not acting, people fantasize about outcomes. This provides "phantom reward" that partially satisfies the motivational system without any behavior occurring. The effect is most damaging at low H, creating a quiet trap where M slowly leaks away. Kappes & Oettingen (2011), J. Experimental Social Psychology

Event System

Life events are modeled as impulses -- instantaneous modifications to state, not gradual rate changes. This reflects the discontinuous nature of real-world events.

Calibration

The model was calibrated against 2,100 synthetic 2-year simulations spanning 21 diverse individuals (ages 19-67, multiple genders, occupations, fitness goals) with 100 event streams each. Event streams were generated via inhomogeneous Poisson processes biased by backstory and trajectory archetypes.

Results were compared against literature-derived targets from Oscarsson et al. (2020), Norcross et al. (2002), Kwasnicka et al. (2016), and Lally et al. (2010). Key metrics like dropout rates at 60 days (63% vs target 50-80%) and activity at 6 months (40% vs target 25-50%) fall within empirical ranges.
