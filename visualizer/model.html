A Dynamical Systems Model of Habit Formation

H-M-E-I-C State Space Model with Literature-Grounded Dynamics

Overview

This model treats habit formation as a trajectory through a 5-dimensional state space. Success and failure emerge from the geometry of the space -- stable attractors (sedentary, established habit) separated by a separatrix. Your trajectory depends on starting conditions, life events, and crucially, the complexity of the behavior you're attempting.

The core thesis: Start with the simplest possible version of the behavior. This keeps you below the separatrix, allowing habit strength to accumulate even as motivation naturally decays. Once automaticity is established, complexity can gradually increase.

State Variables

H Habit Strength — How automatic the behavior has become

Ranges from H_min = 0.02 (no habit, but a residual trace) to 1.0 (fully automatic). Grows asymptotically -- early repetitions matter most, with diminishing returns. Median time to 95% plateau: 66 days (range 18-254). Lally et al. (2010)

As H grows, behavior shifts from needing conscious effort (M-driven) to being triggered automatically by environmental cues. This is the goal-directed → habitual transition.

M Motivational Activation — Current drive to act

The fast variable (hours-days). Ranges from 0 (no motivation) to ~4 (maximum, e.g. after a health scare). Decays hyperbolically -- steep initial drop, long tail. This means a burst of motivation after a New Year's resolution fades quickly in the first week, then slowly over months. Steel & Konig (2006)

E Expectation Excess — Gap between expectations and reality

Positive E = expectations exceed reality ("where are my results?"). Negative E = expectations have been recalibrated downward. E drives two catastrophic failure modes (see dynamics below).

I Identity Coherence — "I am someone who exercises"

The slowest variable (months-years). Only grows when behavior is both occurring AND habitual. Once established, provides a restoring force on M and raises the habit ceiling. The strongest predictor of long-term maintenance (d = 0.73). Rhodes et al. (2016); Berkman et al. (2017)

C Behavioral Complexity — How demanding the target behavior is

Dynamic -- adapts based on feedback. Failure pushes C down (learning to simplify); success pushes it up (progressive overload). Finds an equilibrium proportional to H.

Dynamics: Equation-by-Equation Breakdown

dH/dt Habit Growth & Decay

dH/dt = alpha_H × B × (A(I) - H)  -  decay_rate × (1 - B) × (H - H_min)
Growth term: alpha_H × B × (A(I) - H)
  • alpha_H = 0.045 — habit growth rate. Calibrated so that constant daily behavior (B=1) reaches 95% of ceiling in ~66 days, matching Lally et al. (2010).
  • B — behavioral output (0 to 1). No behavior = no growth. Partial behavior = partial growth.
  • A(I) = A_base + (1 - A_base) × I — the habit ceiling, raised by identity. With A_base=0.8 and I=0, max H is 0.8. With I=0.7 (Carlos), max H rises to 0.94. Identity unlocks higher automaticity.
  • (A(I) - H) — the diminishing returns factor. As H approaches the ceiling, growth slows. First weeks of habit formation produce the most automaticity per repetition.
Decay term: decay_rate × (1 - B) × (H - H_min)
  • decay_rate = delta_H × (1 + 2 × (1-B) × H) — accelerating decay. The base rate is delta_H=0.01, but it increases when behavior drops at high H. A strong habit that stops being practiced decays faster than a weak one. This is "use it or lose it."
  • (1 - B) — decay only occurs when behavior is NOT happening. If B=1 (exercising daily), there's zero decay.
  • H_min = 0.02 — residual floor. Old habits never fully reach zero. Someone who ran for years and stopped still retains a trace of automaticity. Edgren et al. (2025)

dM/dt Motivation Dynamics

Motivation is shaped by many competing forces. Events (shocks, lapses, stress) are applied as impulses -- instantaneous jumps in M -- not through these rate equations.

Hyperbolic decay: -beta_M × M / (1 + M)
beta_M = 0.15 — base decay rate. The / (1 + M) makes this hyperbolic: at high M (e.g. 2.0 after a health scare), decay is fast (~0.1/day); at low M (e.g. 0.2), decay slows to ~0.025/day. This produces the classic pattern: motivation crashes in the first week, then lingers for months as a low hum. Steel & Konig (2006)
Reward prediction error: gamma × B × (1 - H²) × (1 - M/2)
  • gamma = 0.25 — reward coefficient. How much successfully exercising boosts motivation.
  • (1 - H²) — novelty factor. New behavior is exciting and rewarding. As H→1 (fully habitual), the reward signal vanishes. Going to the gym for the first time produces a dopamine hit; going for the 500th time doesn't. Schultz (2016) -- reward prediction error
  • (1 - M/2) — saturation. When M is already high, reward has diminishing marginal utility. You can't get more motivated when you're already at peak motivation.
Effort cost: -lambda × C × (1 - H) × B
  • lambda = 0.2 — effort cost coefficient.
  • C × (1 - H) — effort scales with complexity AND inversely with habit strength. A complex behavior (C=0.8) that isn't habitual yet (H=0.1) costs 0.8 × 0.9 = 0.72 units of effort. A simple behavior (C=0.2) that's somewhat habitual (H=0.4) costs 0.2 × 0.6 = 0.12. This is why high C is devastating early on. Kurzban et al. (2013) -- effort as opportunity cost
Indulgence drain: -gamma_ind × F × (1 - H)
  • gamma_ind = 0.1 — indulgence drain coefficient.
  • F = f_0 × M × (1 - B) — endogenous fantasy. Fires when you're motivated (high M) but not acting (low B). Watching fitness YouTube instead of exercising. The f_0=0.3 parameter controls propensity to fantasize.
  • (1 - H) — state-dependent toxicity. At low H, indulgence is maximally damaging because behavior depends entirely on M. At high H, behavior is cue-driven so fantasy can't substitute for it. Kappes & Oettingen (2011)
False hope collapse: -kappa × softplus(-E - |E_crit|) × M
  • kappa = 0.3 — collapse severity.
  • E_crit = -1.5 — the threshold. When E drops below -1.5 (expectations badly crushed), this term activates and multiplicatively destroys M. softplus is a smooth ramp: zero when E > E_crit, then grows linearly.
  • This produces the false hope cycle: high expectations → reality disappoints → E crashes → M collapses → behavior stops → eventually re-motivate → repeat. Polivy & Herman (2000, 2002)
"What the hell" collapse: -kappa_wth × sigmoid((E - E_wth) / 0.2) × M
  • kappa_wth = 0.8 — collapse severity (stronger than false hope).
  • E_wth = 1.5 — violation threshold. When E exceeds this (you perceive you've broken your goal), the sigmoid switches on and M is rapidly destroyed. The 0.2 denominator makes this a sharp transition -- nearly binary.
  • This is the "I already ate the cake, might as well eat the whole thing" catastrophe. Cochran & Tesser (1996)
Velocity meta-loop: mu × (-dE/dtprev)
mu = 0.1 — when E is decreasing (progress being made), this boosts M. When E is increasing (falling behind expectations), it drains M. This is Carver & Scheier's insight: it's not just the gap that matters, but the rate of gap closure that determines affect. Carver & Scheier (1982, 1998)
Identity restoring force: 0.08 × max(0, 0.2×I - M)
Pulls M up toward a floor proportional to identity. With I=0.7 (Carlos), the floor is 0.14 -- enough to keep M above the activation threshold even after setbacks. With I=0 (Sam), no restoring force at all. This is why identity-stabilized habits survive disruptions.
Habitual maintenance: 0.02 × B × H
Even fully routine behavior generates a small M boost -- the quiet satisfaction of completing a habit. Small (0.02) but persistent, helping maintain M above the activation threshold at equilibrium.
Stress drain: -0.3 × stress × M × (1-H) × (1 - 0.3×social)
  • stress is a transient state (decays with ~10 day half-life) set by stress events.
  • (1-H) — stress mainly impairs goal-directed behavior. Highly habitual behavior is less affected. Schwabe & Wolf (2009)
  • (1 - 0.3×social) — social support buffers stress. With social_support=1.0 (strong gym community), the stress drain is reduced by 30%.
Social support sustain: 0.05 × max(0, 0.15×social - M)
Social support provides a persistent M floor. With social_support=0.8, the floor is 0.12. When M drops below this, social connections gently pull it back up -- the accountability partner who texts "are you coming to class?", the gym buddy who notices when you're absent.

dE/dt Expectation Dynamics

Event impulses add directly to E (shocks inflate expectations; lapses spike them).

Passive drift: phi_M × M × (1 - H)
phi_M = 0.08 — when you're motivated (high M) but haven't built the habit yet (low H), expectations drift upward. "I'm putting in all this effort, surely I should see results." This is the insidious expectation inflation that seeds the false hope cycle.
Progress signal: -psi × B × H
psi = 0.15 — actual progress reduces E. But only when both B and H are present -- because felt progress requires genuine automaticity growth, not just isolated behavioral events. Early effort doesn't feel like progress; a single gym visit doesn't convince you things are working.
Recalibration: -epsilon × E
epsilon = 0.03 — slow natural drift toward E=0. People recalibrate expectations over weeks, but slowly. With epsilon=0.03, the half-life of an expectation excess is ~23 days.

dI/dt Identity Dynamics

dI/dt = alpha_I × B × H × (1 - I)  -  delta_I × (1 - B) × I
Growth: alpha_I × B × H × (1 - I)
alpha_I = 0.005 — very slow. Identity only grows when behavior is both occurring (B>0) AND habitual (H>0). The B×H product means identity doesn't form from isolated attempts or from mere automaticity without action. You become "a runner" by running regularly for months, not by going once or by wanting to go.
Decay: delta_I × (1 - B) × I
delta_I = 0.001 — identity decays 5x slower than it grows. "I used to be a runner" persists for years even after stopping. This asymmetry is why past exercisers recover faster -- the identity scaffold remains even when H has decayed.

dC/dt Complexity Adaptation

C adapts toward an equilibrium determined by current habit strength:

C_equil = C_min + (C_max - C_min) × H × (0.7 + 0.3 × I)

When H=0, C_equil = 0.1 (simplest possible). When H=0.7 with I=0.5, C_equil ≈ 0.61. You can sustain complexity proportional to your automaticity.

Overreach: -alpha_C_down × max(0, C - C_equil) × max(0, 0.3 - B)
alpha_C_down = 0.003 — when C exceeds what H can support AND behavior isn't happening (B < 0.3), C drops. This is the "maybe I should just walk" realization. The max(0, 0.3 - B) means: if you're actually doing the behavior, C doesn't drop even if it's above equilibrium. If it's working, don't fix it.
Progressive overload: alpha_C_up × max(0, C_equil - C) × B
alpha_C_up = 0.001 — when C is below what H can support AND behavior IS happening, C rises. 3x slower than simplification (asymmetric by design -- people simplify faster than they escalate, because failure is louder than success). This produces the natural "I've been walking for months, maybe I should try jogging" progression.
C-M coupling: max(0, -dC/dt) × 0.3
When C is actively dropping (simplifying), M gets a small boost. Lowering the bar makes the goal feel achievable -- "I can walk 15 minutes." This coupling is what makes simplify events so powerful: they don't just reduce C, they re-motivate.

The Behavioral Trigger

B is computed from two competing systems, reflecting the neuroscience of goal-directed vs habitual control: Daw et al. (2005); Dolan & Dayan (2013)

Behavioral threshold: theta = C × (1 - H) + stress × 0.3 × (1 - H) - ii
  • C × (1-H) — the base threshold. High C and low H = high threshold = hard to initiate. For Sam (C=0.9, H=0.02): theta = 0.88. For Tom (C=0.2, H=0.02): theta = 0.20.
  • stress × 0.3 × (1-H) — stress raises the threshold for non-habitual behavior (impairs prefrontal control). But doesn't affect habitual behavior (1-H → 0). Schwabe & Wolf (2009)
  • ii — implementation intention effect. Directly lowers the threshold. Gollwitzer (1999)
Two-component blend
B_goal = sigmoid((M - theta) / tau)
B_habit = H × sigmoid((M - M_floor) / 0.08)
B = [(1 - H1.5) × B_goal + H1.5 × B_habit] × activation_gate
  • tau = 0.15 — softness of the goal-directed threshold. Smaller = sharper on/off.
  • B_goal: sigmoid of M vs threshold. When M > theta, you'll probably exercise today.
  • B_habit: H itself drives behavior, but requires a minimum M that shrinks as H grows. Even at H=0.8, you need some motivation -- human habits are never fully automatic. de Wit et al. (2018)
  • H1.5 blend: at low H, mostly goal-directed; at high H, mostly habitual. The 1.5 exponent makes the transition gradual but accelerating.
  • Activation gate: sigmoid((M - 0.08) / 0.03) — kills B completely when M < 0.08. You don't accidentally exercise. This ensures the sedentary state is a true fixed point.
