## 1. Identity and Self-Concept in Behavior Change

### 1.1 Exercise Identity and Long-Term Maintenance

**Key claim:** Exercise identity -- the degree to which one sees oneself as "an exerciser" -- is the strongest correlate of translating physical activity intentions into action (d = 0.73), and identity change predicts an additional ~16 min/week of exercise at follow-up. Identity and habit are proposed as the two core "reflexive processes" that reduce reliance on effortful self-regulation for behavioral maintenance.

**Citations:**
- Rhodes, R. E., & Sui, W. (2021). Physical Activity Maintenance: A Critical Narrative Review and Directions for Future Research. *Frontiers in Psychology*, 12, 725671. DOI: 10.3389/fpsyg.2021.725671. PMID: 34552537.
- Rhodes, R. E., Kaushal, N., & Quinlan, A. (2016). Is physical activity a part of who I am? A review and meta-analysis of identity, schema and physical activity. *Health Psychology Review*, 10(2), 204-225. DOI: 10.1080/17437199.2016.1143334. PMID: 26805431.
- Rhodes, R. E., et al. (2025). Changes in identity and habit formation during 3 months of sport and physical activity participation among parents with young children. *Applied Psychology: Health and Well-Being*. DOI: 10.1111/aphw.70009.

**Model assessment:** PARTIALLY CAPTURED. The model's H variable captures automaticity, but identity is conceptually distinct from habit -- it provides a self-regulatory "shield" that protects behavior even when habits are disrupted. Identity should be a separate slow variable (I) that feeds back into M and H.

### 1.2 Identity-Based Motivation (IBM) Theory

**Key claim:** People prefer to act in identity-congruent ways (action-readiness), identities are dynamically constructed in context despite feeling stable, and when behavior feels identity-congruent, difficulty is interpreted as indicating importance rather than impossibility. Three core principles: (1) action-readiness, (2) dynamic construction, (3) interpretation of difficulty.

**Citations:**
- Oyserman, D., & Destin, M. (2010). Identity-based motivation: Implications for intervention. *The Counseling Psychologist*, 38(7), 1001-1043. DOI: 10.1177/0011000010374775. PMID: 21516204.
- Oyserman, D. (2015). Identity-based motivation. In *Emerging Trends in the Social and Behavioral Sciences*. Wiley.
- Oyserman, D., Elmore, K., & Smith, G. (2012). Self, self-concept, and identity. In M. Leary & J. Tangney (Eds.), *Handbook of Self and Identity* (2nd ed.). Guilford Press.

**Model assessment:** MISSED. The current model has no mechanism for identity-congruence to modulate how difficulty/setbacks (E) are interpreted. In IBM theory, the same E could be motivating (if identity-congruent) or demoralizing (if identity-incongruent). This is a qualitative shift the model cannot represent without an identity variable.

### 1.3 Temporal Dynamics of Self-Concept

**Key claim:** Self-concept shows moderate stability with domain-specific variability. Longitudinal studies find ~50% of individuals show stable identity commitments while ~50% show a mid-adolescent dip. Identity can change but operates on slower timescales than behavior, making it a genuine "slow variable" in dynamical systems terms. Self-concept clarity increases gradually over adolescence and early adulthood.

**Citations:**
- Klimstra, T. A., et al. (2010). Identity formation in adolescence: Change or stability? *Journal of Youth and Adolescence*, 39, 150-162.
- Demo, D. H. (1992). The Self-Concept Over Time: Research Issues and Directions. *Annual Review of Sociology*, 18, 303-326.
- Sedikides, C., & Wildschut, T. (2018). On the temporal navigation of selfhood: The role of self-continuity. *Self and Identity*, 17(6), 648-669. DOI: 10.1080/15298868.2017.1391115.

**Model assessment:** SUPPORTS ADDING I AS SLOW VARIABLE. The empirical evidence confirms that identity changes on timescales of months-to-years, much slower than daily habit performance or weekly motivation fluctuations. This validates modeling identity as dI/dt with a small coefficient.

### 1.4 Self-Schema Theory and Behavior

**Key claim:** Exerciser self-schematics process exercise-related information faster, recall more past exercise behavior, are more committed to future exercise, and use more strategies to maintain exercise on difficult days. Having an "exerciser schema" predicts adoption of exercise programs above and beyond current behavior.

**Citations:**
- Kendzierski, D. (1988). Self-schemata and exercise. *Basic and Applied Social Psychology*, 9(1), 45-59. DOI: 10.1207/s15324834basp0901_4.
- Kendzierski, D. (1990). Exercise self-schemata: Cognitive and behavioral correlates. *Health Psychology*, 9(1), 69-82. DOI: 10.1037/0278-6133.9.1.69. PMID: 2323330.

**Model assessment:** MISSED. Self-schemata operate as information-processing filters that bias attention, memory, and planning toward schema-consistent behavior. This is a cognitive infrastructure that the current model does not represent. It aligns with the proposed identity variable but adds a specific mechanism: schematic processing reduces cognitive load, similar to but distinct from automaticity (H).

### 1.5 The Identity-Value Model (IVM) of Self-Regulation

**Key claim:** Identity increases the subjective value of identity-congruent behaviors, making them more likely to win the value-integration competition against temptations. This operates via ventromedial prefrontal cortex (vmPFC), the same region involved in valuation. As behavior becomes part of one's identity, it requires less effortful self-regulation (the "Maintain IT" model). Five dimensions of "centered identity": behavior-based identity, role/group identities, personal values, perceptual lens, and psychological well-being.

**Citations:**
- Berkman, E. T., Livingston, J. L., & Kahn, L. E. (2017). Finding the "self" in self-regulation: The identity-value model. *Psychological Inquiry*, 28(2-3), 77-98. DOI: 10.1080/1047840X.2017.1323463. PMID: 30774280.
- Berkman, E. T., Livingston, J. L., & Kahn, L. E. (2017). The identity-value model of self-regulation: Integration, extension, and open questions. *Psychological Inquiry*, 28(2-3), 157-164. DOI: 10.1080/1047840X.2017.1343069. PMID: 30774281.
- Caldwell, A. E., et al. (2018). Harnessing Centered Identity Transformation to Reduce Executive Function Burden for Maintenance of Health Behavior Change: The Maintain IT Model. *Health Psychology Review*, 12(1), 35-69. DOI: 10.1080/17437199.2018.1437551. PMID: 29402182.

**Model assessment:** MISSED BUT PROVIDES FORMALISM. The IVM gives a concrete mechanism: I (identity) modulates the effective value of the target behavior, which could be incorporated as I amplifying M or reducing the threshold for action. The Maintain IT model specifically predicts that identity transformation reduces executive function burden over time -- suggesting dH/dt should depend on I.

## 2. Social and Environmental Factors

### 2.1 Social Contagion of Health Behaviors

**Key claim:** In the Framingham Heart Study network (12,067 people, 32 years), a friend becoming obese increased one's own risk by 57%. Among mutual friends, the effect was 171%. The influence extended to three degrees of separation. Similar contagion effects were found for smoking cessation, happiness, and exercise behaviors.

**Note on methodology:** These findings have been critiqued for potential confounding by shared environments and homophily (Cohen-Cole & Fletcher, 2008, *Journal of Health Economics*). The social contagion effect is likely real but its magnitude may be overstated.

**Citations:**
- Christakis, N. A., & Fowler, J. H. (2007). The spread of obesity in a large social network over 32 years. *New England Journal of Medicine*, 357(4), 370-379. DOI: 10.1056/NEJMsa066082. PMID: 17652652.
- Christakis, N. A., & Fowler, J. H. (2008). The collective dynamics of smoking in a large social network. *New England Journal of Medicine*, 358(21), 2249-2258.

**Model assessment:** MISSED. The model is purely individual. Social influence could enter as an exogenous coupling term or a separate social norm variable S(t) that modulates M and provides a reference point for E.

### 2.2 Accountability, Coaching, and Social Support

**Key claim:** Human support increases adherence through accountability to a coach perceived as trustworthy and expert. "Autonomous accountability" (internally motivated) is more effective long-term than "controlled accountability" (shame-based). Weekly reporting increased treatment adherence by >100% in some contexts. Exercise coaching significantly improves adherence through self-efficacy enhancement and barrier management.

**Citations:**
- Oussedik, E., et al. (2017). Accountability: A missing construct in models of adherence behavior and in clinical practice. *Patient Preference and Adherence*, 11, 1285-1294. DOI: 10.2147/PPA.S135895.
- Mohr, D. C., Cuijpers, P., & Lehman, K. (2011). Supportive accountability: A model for providing human support to enhance adherence to eHealth interventions. *Journal of Medical Internet Research*, 13(1), e30. DOI: 10.2196/jmir.1602.

**Model assessment:** MISSED. Accountability creates an external reference signal that modifies the effective cost of not performing the behavior. This could be modeled as a social coupling term in dM/dt or as modifying the "decay" rate of M when behavior is skipped.

### 2.3 Environmental Design and Choice Architecture

**Key claim:** Small changes in the choice environment ("nudges") predictably alter behavior without restricting options. Key tools include defaults, feedback, structured choices, and incentive framing. For habits specifically, environmental restructuring (e.g., placing gym clothes by the door) reduces friction and supports cue-dependent automaticity.

**Citations:**
- Thaler, R. H., & Sunstein, C. R. (2008). *Nudge: Improving Decisions about Health, Wealth, and Happiness*. Yale University Press.
- Thaler, R. H., Sunstein, C. R., & Balz, J. P. (2013). Choice architecture. In E. Shafir (Ed.), *The Behavioral Foundations of Public Policy*. Princeton University Press. SSRN: 1583509.

**Model assessment:** PARTIALLY CAPTURED. Environmental factors influence the cue-response link in H, but the model has no explicit "friction" or "environmental support" parameter. Adding a context variable C(t) that modulates both the ease of habit execution and the strength of cues would capture this.

### 2.4 Habit Discontinuity Hypothesis

**Key claim:** Life transitions (moving house, changing jobs, retirement) temporarily disrupt habitual patterns, creating a "window of opportunity" lasting approximately 3 months during which behavior change interventions are significantly more effective. This is because context changes break cue-response associations.

**Citations:**
- Verplanken, B., & Roy, D. (2016). Empowering interventions to promote sustainable lifestyles: Testing the habit discontinuity hypothesis in a field experiment. *Journal of Environmental Psychology*, 45, 127-134. DOI: 10.1016/j.jenvp.2015.11.008.
- Verplanken, B., Walker, I., Davis, A., & Jurasek, M. (2008). Context change and travel mode choice: Combining the habit discontinuity and self-activation hypotheses. *Journal of Environmental Psychology*, 28(2), 121-127.

**Model assessment:** PARTIALLY CAPTURED. If H represents cue-dependent automaticity, then a context disruption would reset H toward zero. But the model needs to distinguish "habit strength in context" from "latent skill/knowledge." A person who moves house loses cue-associations but not the ability to exercise. This suggests H should have both a context-dependent component and a context-independent component.

## 3. Goal Systems and Self-Regulation Theory

### 3.1 Goal Systems Theory

**Key claim:** Goals and means are organized in interconnected hierarchical networks. Key structural features include equifinality (multiple means to one goal), multifinality (one means serving multiple goals), and counterfinality (means that serve one goal but undermine another). Habitual behavior is goal-driven -- habits are not purposeless but are attached to goals, though sometimes different goals than originally intended.

**Citations:**
- Kruglanski, A. W., et al. (2002). A theory of goal systems. *Advances in Experimental Social Psychology*, 34, 331-378. DOI: 10.1016/S0065-2601(02)80008-9.
- Kruglanski, A. W., & Szumowska, E. (2020). Habitual behavior is goal-driven. *Perspectives on Psychological Science*, 15(5), 1256-1271. DOI: 10.1177/1745691620917676. PMID: 32569529.
- Kruglanski, A. W., Fishbach, A., & Kopetz, C. (2023). *Goal Systems Theory: Psychological Processes and Applications*. Oxford University Press.

**Model assessment:** PARTIALLY CAPTURED. M represents motivation but not the hierarchical structure of goals. The model cannot represent equifinality (substituting means), multifinality (one behavior serving multiple goals), or goal conflict. A richer representation might include a goal-relevance weight that connects H to multiple goal nodes.

---

### 3.2 Cybernetic Models of Self-Regulation (Carver & Scheier)

**Key claim:** Behavior is regulated by negative feedback loops that compare current state to a reference value (goal) and act to minimize discrepancy. A meta-monitoring loop tracks the *rate* of discrepancy reduction, generating affect: positive affect when progress exceeds expectations, negative affect when progress falls short. This meta-loop governs goal engagement vs. disengagement.

**Citations:**
- Carver, C. S., & Scheier, M. F. (1982). Control theory: A useful conceptual framework for personality-social, clinical, and health psychology. *Psychological Bulletin*, 92(1), 111-135. DOI: 10.1037/0033-2909.92.1.111. PMID: 7134324.
- Carver, C. S. (2018). Control theory, goal attainment, and psychopathology. *Psychological Inquiry*, 29(3), 139-144. DOI: 10.1080/1047840X.2018.1513681. PMID: 31447528.
- Carver, C. S., & Scheier, M. F. (1998). *On the Self-Regulation of Behavior*. Cambridge University Press.

**Model assessment:** WELL ALIGNED. The current model's E (expectation gap) is essentially the discrepancy signal in a Carver-Scheier feedback loop. However, the model may be missing the *velocity* meta-loop -- the rate of change of E, not just E itself, drives affect and engagement/disengagement decisions. Adding dE/dt as a signal that modifies M would capture this.

### 3.3 Perceptual Control Theory (Powers)

**Key claim:** Organisms control their *perceptions*, not their behavior. Behavior is the means by which perceptions are brought into alignment with internal reference values. The control hierarchy has ~11 levels, from intensity sensing up to system concepts. Higher levels set reference values for lower levels.

**Citations:**
- Powers, W. T. (1973). *Behavior: The Control of Perception*. Aldine de Gruyter.
- Powers, W. T. (2005). *Behavior: The Control of Perception* (2nd ed.). Benchmark Publications.

**Model assessment:** CONCEPTUALLY RELEVANT. Powers' hierarchy suggests that the model should distinguish levels of control. Identity (system concept level) sets reference values for goal-level control, which sets reference values for program-level control (habits). This provides a theoretical justification for the hierarchical relationship: I -> M -> H.

### 3.4 Planning, Self-Monitoring, and Implementation Intentions

**Key claim:** Implementation intentions ("if X happens, then I will do Y") have a medium-to-large effect on goal attainment (d = 0.65 across 94 studies). They work by creating heightened perceptual readiness for the specified cue and a strong associative link to the planned response -- essentially, they create proto-habits through a single cognitive act. Self-monitoring is consistently one of the most effective behavior change techniques.

**Citations:**
- Gollwitzer, P. M. (1999). Implementation intentions: Strong effects of simple plans. *American Psychologist*, 54(7), 493-503.
- Gollwitzer, P. M., & Sheeran, P. (2006). Implementation intentions and goal achievement: A meta-analysis of effects and processes. *Advances in Experimental Social Psychology*, 38, 69-119.

**Model assessment:** PARTIALLY CAPTURED. Implementation intentions accelerate the formation of cue-response associations, which maps onto an increased dH/dt. But the model doesn't explicitly represent planning as a distinct process that bootstraps H formation. Self-monitoring maps onto the accuracy of perceiving the current state in the feedback loop.

## 4. Phenomena the Current Model Might Miss

### 4.1 Stress-Habit Interactions

**Key claim:** Acute stress shifts behavioral control from goal-directed (prefrontal cortex, flexible) to habitual (dorsal striatum, inflexible). This is mediated by simultaneous glucocorticoid and noradrenergic activation. Stressed subjects continued selecting devalued actions at the same rate as valued ones, unlike controls. Only 28% of stressed participants could name action-outcome associations vs. 58% of controls.

**Citations:**
- Schwabe, L., & Wolf, O. T. (2009). Stress prompts habit behavior in humans. *Journal of Neuroscience*, 29(22), 7191-7198. DOI: 10.1523/JNEUROSCI.0979-09.2009. PMID: 19494141.
- Schwabe, L., & Wolf, O. T. (2011). Stress-induced modulation of instrumental behavior: From goal-directed to habitual control of action. *Behavioural Brain Research*, 219(2), 321-328.
- Heatherton, T. F., & Wagner, D. D. (2011). Cognitive neuroscience of self-regulation failure. *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*, 15(3), 132-139. DOI: 10.1016/j.tics.2010.12.005. PMID: 21273114.

**Model assessment:** CRITICAL GAP. Under stress, the model should predict that H dominates over M (goal-directed motivation). A stress variable sigma(t) should modulate the relative influence of H vs. M on behavior. When sigma is high, behavior follows H regardless of M or E. Important implication: if H is a good habit, stress helps; if H is a bad habit, stress causes relapse.

---

### 4.2 Sleep, Circadian Rhythms, and Self-Regulation Capacity

**Key claim:** Sleep deprivation impairs prefrontal cortex function, reducing self-control capacity. Poor sleep reduces blood glucose utilization (needed for executive function), increases emotional reactivity, and biases individuals toward easier tasks. Self-control shows circadian variation, being highest after adequate sleep and declining through the day.

**Citations:**
- Pilcher, J. J., et al. (2015). Interactions between sleep habits and self-control. *Frontiers in Human Neuroscience*, 9, 284. DOI: 10.3389/fnhum.2015.00284. PMID: 26029094.
- Lim, J., & Dinges, D. F. (2010). A meta-analysis of the impact of short-term sleep deprivation on cognitive variables. *Psychological Bulletin*, 136(3), 375-389.

**Model assessment:** MISSED. Sleep/circadian effects could be modeled as a time-varying capacity parameter R(t) that modulates the effectiveness of M (conscious motivation) but not H (automatic habits). Consistent with the stress-habit interaction: when capacity is low, habitual behavior dominates.

---

### 4.3 Competing Habits and Behavioral Substitution

**Key claim:** Breaking a bad habit is most effective when a competing "good" habit is installed using the same cue. Habit reversal training (awareness + competing response) is effective across domains. The brain maintains two parallel systems (S-R and goal-directed) that compete for behavioral control. Breaking habits requires weakening S-R links, avoiding triggers, goal-directed inhibition, or forming competing S-R associations.

**Citations:**
- Wood, W., & Runger, D. (2016). Psychology of habit. *Annual Review of Psychology*, 67, 289-314. DOI: 10.1146/annurev-psych-122414-033417. PMID: 26361052.
- Gardner, B. (2024). What is habit and how can it be used to change real-world behaviour? *Social and Personality Psychology Compass*, 18, e12975.

**Model assessment:** MISSED. The model has a single H variable. In reality, multiple habits compete for expression in the same context. The model should either: (a) represent H as a vector with competing components, or (b) include a "bad habit" variable H_bad that competes with H_good. Net behavior = f(H_good, H_bad, M, sigma).

### 4.4 Plateau Effects

**Key claim:** Habit automaticity follows an asymptotic curve, reaching a plateau after an average of 66 days (range: 18-254 days). Weight loss plateaus typically begin ~6 months in, driven by both metabolic adaptation (reduced BMR) and adherence decline. The psychological impact of plateaus includes discouragement and dropout.

**Citations:**
- Lally, P., et al. (2010). How are habits formed: Modelling habit formation in the real world. *European Journal of Social Psychology*, 40(6), 998-1009. DOI: 10.1002/ejsp.674.
- Thomas, D. M., et al. (2014). Effect of dietary adherence on the body weight plateau: A mathematical model incorporating intermittent compliance with energy intake prescription. *American Journal of Clinical Nutrition*, 100(3), 787-795. DOI: 10.3945/ajcn.113.079822.

**Model assessment:** PARTIALLY CAPTURED. If dH/dt follows diminishing returns, the model naturally produces a plateau in H. However, the motivational consequences of the plateau are not captured. When dH/dt slows (or fitness gains plateau), E increases (outcomes diverge from expectations), which should feed back negatively on M. This expectation-reality gap during plateaus is a key dropout trigger.

---

### 4.5 The "What the Hell" Effect (Counter-Regulatory Eating)

**Key claim:** When goal-setters perceive they have already violated their goal, they experience a cognitive shift that leads to complete abandonment of restraint. This is especially pronounced with short-term, all-or-nothing goal framing. Inhibitory goals benefit from long-term framing; flexible approaches reduce the effect.

**Citations:**
- Cochran, W., & Tesser, A. (1996). The "what the hell" effect: Some effects of goal proximity and goal framing on performance. In L. Martin & A. Tesser (Eds.), *Striving and Feeling: Interactions Among Goals, Affect, and Self-Regulation*. Lawrence Erlbaum Associates.
- Polivy, J., & Herman, C. P. (1985). Dieting and binging: A causal analysis. *American Psychologist*, 40(2), 193-201.

**Model assessment:** CRITICAL GAP. The current model likely treats goal violation as a linear decrease in M. But the "what the hell" effect is a *nonlinear collapse* -- once E exceeds a threshold (perceived violation), M drops catastrophically rather than gradually. This could be modeled as a bifurcation or threshold effect in the M dynamics: dM/dt has a discontinuity when E crosses a critical value.

---

### 4.6 Habit Stacking / Cue Chaining

**Key claim:** Habit stacking leverages existing habits as cues for new ones ("After I [current habit], I will [new habit]"). BJ Fogg's Tiny Habits method uses the format "After I [Anchor Moment], I will [Tiny Behavior]" and emphasizes celebration (positive emotion) to reinforce the new link. Works because existing habits are reliable cues with high temporal and contextual consistency.

**Citations:**
- Fogg, B. J. (2020). *Tiny Habits: The Small Changes That Change Everything*. Houghton Mifflin Harcourt.
- Clear, J. (2018). *Atomic Habits*. Avery/Penguin. (Popularization drawing on Fogg and Gollwitzer.)

**Model assessment:** MISSED. The model treats H as a single variable, but habit stacking creates *networks* of habits where the completion of one triggers the next. Could be modeled as coupled H variables: H_1 completing increases the cue strength for H_2. The chain's weakest link determines overall robustness.

## 5. Summary: Proposed Model Extensions

| Phenomenon | Current Coverage | Suggested Extension |
|---|---|---|
| Identity/self-concept | Not captured | Add slow variable I(t), dI/dt ~ f(H, behavior frequency) |
| Social influence | Not captured | Add social coupling S(t) or exogenous social norm parameter |
| Stress-habit shift | Not captured | Add stress sigma(t) that shifts control from M to H |
| Sleep/circadian capacity | Not captured | Add time-varying capacity parameter R(t) modulating M |
| Competing habits | Not captured | Extend H to vector or add H_bad competing variable |
| Goal hierarchy | Not captured | Consider M as deriving from higher-order goal structure |
| What-the-hell effect | Not captured | Add threshold/bifurcation in M dynamics when E > E_crit |
| Habit stacking | Not captured | Model coupled H variables with cue-chaining |
| Velocity of progress | Partially captured | Add dE/dt as input to M dynamics (Carver-Scheier meta-loop) |
| Environmental context | Partially captured | Add context variable C(t) modulating H expression |
| Plateau effects | Partially captured | Already emerges from diminishing returns in dH/dt |
| Implementation intentions | Partially captured | Maps to accelerated dH/dt formation |

### Priority Additions (by theoretical importance and parsimony):

1. **Identity variable I(t)** -- supported by Rhodes, Oyserman, Berkman, Kendzierski, Caldwell. Slow variable, feeds back into M and H. Most strongly supported addition.
2. **Stress/capacity variable sigma(t) or R(t)** -- supported by Schwabe & Wolf, Heatherton & Wagner, Pilcher et al. Modulates the M-vs-H balance. Critical for modeling relapse.
3. **Social coupling term** -- supported by Christakis & Fowler, Oussedik et al. Can be a parameter rather than a full state variable.
4. **Nonlinear threshold in M dynamics** -- supported by Cochran & Tesser, Polivy & Herman. Captures catastrophic motivation collapse.
5. **Velocity meta-loop (dE/dt -> M)** -- supported by Carver & Scheier. Small modification with high theoretical payoff.
