Cochran, W., & Tesser, A. (1996).
The "what the hell" effect: Some effects of goal proximity and goal framing on performance.
In L. Martin & A. Tesser (Eds.), Striving and Feeling: Interactions Among Goals,
Affect, and Self-Regulation. Lawrence Erlbaum Associates.

Also:
Polivy, J., & Herman, C. P. (1985).
Dieting and binging: A causal analysis.
American Psychologist, 40(2), 193-201.

KEY FINDINGS:
- When goal-setters perceive they have already violated their goal, they experience
  a cognitive shift leading to complete abandonment of restraint
- "What the hell, I already blew it" -- all-or-nothing response to perceived violation
- Especially pronounced with short-term, all-or-nothing goal framing
- Begins with failing a goal or breaking a streak -> guilt -> overindulging to soothe guilt
- Counter-regulatory eating: restrained eaters who perceive they have exceeded their
  diet boundary eat MORE than unrestrained eaters
- Inhibitory goals benefit from long-term framing
- Flexible approaches (allowing small portions of wide range of foods) reduce the effect
- MODEL IMPLICATION: Nonlinear/catastrophic collapse in motivation, not gradual decline
  Suggests bifurcation when E > E_critical
