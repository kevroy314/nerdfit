Nerdfit

Nerds are, by our nature, odd. We like details. We like to understand and ask why. We ruin board game nights by finding the most esoteric routes to winning. We annoy our family members on easter by giving an extensive history of the differentiation between rogue-like and rogue-lite genras of games. We can call out specific episodes of Star Trek by name. We slip into idle conversation the fact thatmMore people have walked on the moon (12) than have scored an earned run off Mariano Rivera in the postseason (11). We're known for being smart, thoughtful, detail oriented, obsessive, and occasionally insufferable about our passions. But we're not known for having hot bods.

The modern world has largely rejected the jock vs. nerd dynamic. Jocks are just sports nerds - we all see that now. Being a nerd is about the passion for the subject, but there's one divide between jocks and nerds that has yet to have been fully bridged. Sports nerds start good exercise habits from a young age. DND nerds, unfortunately, rarely do. But regardless of your age, if you're a nerd and are willing, you can still chose to nerd out about fitness, nutrition, and positive habit formation in your life. It could be because you want to live longer or look hotter (you deserve to feel like you look hot), it could be because you want to channel your inner batman, or it could be because you need to do something to offset the 8 bawls and far too many doritos locos tacos over the weekend. Your reasons are your own, but the question is whether or not you're willing to embrace these topics (and habit formation in general) as just another thing you have the opportunity to nerd out about.

The Game Guide: Boostrapping

Ok that was fun, but I've probably only told you things you already know (with the exception of maybe thinking more carefully about the value you derive from the occasional vice). How do you actually level up? Let's illustrate by taking an aside to learn about the concept of Boostrapping. 

Have you ever wondered how we wrote the first computer programs? I'm talking about before we even had keyboards (how would you have programmed the keyboard? or the thing that reads it?). Even before monitors or the software that displays the letters. Like, how did we make those without them already existing so we had something to make them on? The answer is boostrapping. Computers have, by-and-large, an unbroken lineage back to the earliest pencil and paper "flip the switches" style programming. We used the simpler computers to help us make the better computers. We wrote the first C compiler in assembly. We wrote the second one in C. If we had tried, in the early days of computing, to jump straight to writing a modern operating system, we would've failed catastrophically. We didn't have the tools to do it. If you're early-ish in your fitness journey, and you're expecting to jump straight into the deep end, you'ore going to have an equally bad time. Fitness, nutrition, and "good" habits are skills. You build them over time with one simple goal: a habit or skill should become easy (automatic, in the habit forming terms). It shouldn't be effortful. If it's still effortful, the habit isn't fully formed and the skill isn't fully learned.

Traps

If you generally accept the above, you should know a variety of terrible traps await you on your journey. I can't name them all; we nerds are inventive, and I'm sure you'll all come up with creative new ones. But the following are some of the most common traps. Note that these are pretty much exclusively mental traps. This is a mental game - a mental skillset, not a physical one.

The Trick Trap
This one is easy to state and deseperately hard to internalize: there are no tricks or shortcuts. If you're hunting for a way to make it "easier", you're probably doing something wrong. Yes, you need to log your macros if you want to reliably lose weight. Yes, you need to work out several days a week. No, you can't just take drugs to make it all easier. Does that mean there's no hope to make everything easier? Not at all! But you have to earn that ease with hard work. You can't skip the hard work. Anyone who tells you you can is selling you something.

The "What The Hell" Trap
You've set a goal, but your expectations are somehow severely violated, you'll often experience a hard crash out. This is "all-or-nothing" thinking, and it's one of the easiest failure modes. The lighter version, licensing, is the idea that you'll trade every good choice you made for a bad choice that feels good. You did your work out? You get a cookie. That's licensing. You at a pile of cookies so you're giving up on your fitness goals? That's the "What the Hell" effect.

Phantom Rewards
This one is tough. You've convinced yourself this fitness and nutrition thing is for you this time. You spend all day thinking about how you're going to get in that after work workout. Then, you're exhausted at the end of the day and you don't do it. Unfortunately, that day worth of mental energy you spent thinking about working out "counted" to your brain. Repeat that and suddenly it feels like you've been spending a lot of mental energy on fitness, but you've spent vanishingly few actual minutes in the gym. Thinking about a thing isn't work. It doesn't count. Don't let your brain trick you.

Complexity Overshooting
More subtle than the above, a highly motiviated individual might spend a lot of time in the gym and counting calories. They might put in a hard month. How much weight should they have lost in that extremely intense month? Well, generally, you're not going to lose much more than a pound a week. So all that intense effort might have gotten you 5 lbs off. Was it worth it? No, of course not. You overshot the difficulty curve by having your actions be way too complex. Now you're exhausted and just want it to end. Another way to say this is "slow and steady wins the race", but it's more than that. You should push yourself, but only as hard as is sustainable and goal-matching. If your expectations are well aligned to your efforts, you can work extra hard and it pays off. But if you don't know what to expect because this is your first time losing a lot of weight, that complexity overshoot can be a painful wake up call.

Life-Induced Failure
Even if you manage to avoid all of the above, life happens. You're going to get sick, have a bad week at work, go on a fun trip, or something else. That's going to set you back from you goals. You can't (or, maybe, shouldn't) try to stop these things from happening completely. But on that first day back from your trip, on that weekend after your bad week, or on that day you're feeling a little better - did you eat well? Did you hit the gym? Did you "get back on the horse"?
