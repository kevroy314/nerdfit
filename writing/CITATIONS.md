## Citation Strategy

This is a Substack article, not a paper. Citations should be:
- **Inline hyperlinks** on the relevant claim, not numbered footnotes
- **Selective** -- cite the surprising, the counterintuitive, and the specific. Don't cite common knowledge.
- **Author-named where the person matters** -- e.g., "Oettingen calls this a phantom reward" is better than a bare link
- **Collected at the bottom** in a "Further Reading" or "Sources" section for the curious

#### Phenomena Enumeration (for the TODO list)

When you build the phenomena list, here's what to cite for each:

| Phenomenon | One-liner | Citation |
|-----------|-----------|----------|
| **Habit automaticity curve** | Habits follow an asymptotic curve, not linear growth. Average 66 days, range 18-254. | Lally et al. (2010); Singh et al. (2024) |
| **Habit decay** | Habit strength decays on a decelerating curve, stabilizing around 9-10 days of non-practice. | Edgren et al. (2025) |
| **Phantom rewards** | Fantasizing about success makes your brain think you already succeeded, draining motivation. | Kappes & Oettingen (2011) |
| **What-the-hell effect** | One violation triggers catastrophic abandonment, not gradual decline. | Cochran & Tesser (1996); Polivy & Herman (1985) |
| **False hope syndrome** | Unrealistic expectations → failure → reinterpretation → new unrealistic attempt. A cycle. | Polivy & Herman (2002) |
| **Stress → habit shift** | Acute stress pushes you from goal-directed to habitual behavior. Can be protective or destructive. | Schwabe & Wolf (2009) |
| **Dopamine prediction error** | Rewards you expect produce no dopamine. Only surprises do. Habits become "invisible" to reward systems. | Schultz (2016) |
| **Implementation intentions** | "If X, then Y" plans produce d=0.65 effect size. One cognitive act creates a proto-habit. | Gollwitzer (1999); Gollwitzer & Sheeran (2006) |
| **Social contagion** | A friend becoming obese increases your risk by 57%. Mutual friends: 171%. | Christakis & Fowler (2007) |
| **Fresh start effect** | Gym visits spike 33% on Mondays, 47% at semester starts. Temporal landmarks create motivational resets. | Dai et al. (2014) |
| **Relapse prevention** | High-risk situation → poor coping → decreased self-efficacy → lapse → guilt → full relapse. | Marlatt (Larimer et al., 1999) |
| **Velocity feedback** | Your brain tracks the *rate* of progress, not just progress itself. Slowing down feels bad even if you're ahead. | Carver & Scheier (1982) |
| **Effort as opportunity cost** | "Willpower depletion" is really your brain computing that your effort would be better spent elsewhere. | Kurzban et al. (2013) |
| **Identity-behavior feedback** | Identity predicts maintenance (d=0.73). Exercise identity → more exercise → stronger identity. Virtuous cycle. | Rhodes & Sui (2021); Stojanovic et al. (2021) |
| **Habit discontinuity** | Life transitions (moving, new job) break old habits AND create windows for new ones. ~3 month window. | Verplanken & Roy (2016) |
| **Sleep-self-control link** | Sleep deprivation directly impairs PFC function. Even high motivation can't compensate. | Pilcher et al. (2015) |
| **Dual-mode exercise affect** | Below ventilatory threshold: exercise feels fine. Above it: exercise feels bad. +1 unit of in-task feeling = +38 min/week at 6 months. | Ekkekakis (2009) |
| **New Year's resolution decay** | Success drops from 88.8% (January) to 54.7% (December). Approach goals beat avoidance goals. | Oscarsson et al. (2020) |
| **Accountability** | Weekly reporting increases adherence >100% in some contexts. Autonomous > shame-based. | Oussedik et al. (2017) |
| **Goal-habit arbitration** | Brain runs goal-directed and habitual systems in parallel, arbitrating by uncertainty. | Daw et al. (2005); Dolan & Dayan (2013) |
| **Fragile human habits** | Unlike rat habits, human habits may never become fully goal-independent. Motivation always matters somewhat. | de Wit et al. (2018) |

## Notes on Citation Density

For a Substack article of this length (~2,500-3,500 words when complete), aim for:
- **8-12 inline hyperlinks** in the body text, placed on surprising claims
- **A "Sources & Further Reading" section** at the bottom with the full list (readers who want to go deeper will appreciate it; everyone else will scroll past)
- **Name-drop 3-5 researchers** in the text itself where it adds flavor (Galen, Lavoisier, Oettingen for phantom rewards, Lally for the 66-day number, Carver & Scheier for velocity feedback)
- **Don't cite common knowledge** -- "protein builds muscle" doesn't need a citation in a Substack post. "Fantasizing about success literally lowers your blood pressure and saps your motivation" does.
